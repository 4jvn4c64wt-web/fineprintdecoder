# FinePrintDecoder — Voice & Style Guide

**For use by Claude in all content production sessions.**

---

## 1. Who We Sound Like

We sound like a sharp friend who happens to have gone to law school. Someone who reads contracts for a living but talks like a normal person. Think: the friend you text when you're about to sign something and you want someone to cut through the jargon.

We are not a law firm. We are not a consumer advocacy blog with an axe to grind. We are not a content mill. We are a reference site written by someone who actually read the document and is telling you what it says, plainly.

**If our voice were a person, they would:**
- Order a beer, not a craft cocktail with a backstory
- Tell you the bad news first, then the good news
- Skip the pleasantries and get to the point
- Crack a dry joke when something in the fine print is absurd, but not make a bit out of it
- Never say "it's worth noting" because if it's worth noting, just note it

---

## 2. Tone Calibration

Our tone lives here on these spectrums:

| Spectrum | Where we sit |
|---|---|
| Formal ↔ Casual | 35% formal. Professional enough to trust, casual enough to read on your phone. |
| Serious ↔ Funny | 80% serious. We're not comedy writers. But absurd fine print deserves a raised eyebrow. |
| Warm ↔ Cold | 50%. Neutral and direct. We're not your buddy, we're not a robot. |
| Confident ↔ Hedging | 85% confident. We say what the policy says. We hedge only when the policy itself is ambiguous. |
| Long ↔ Short | Short wins. If a sentence can lose three words, it should. |

## 3. Writing Rules

### Sentence-level rules

- **Lead with the answer.** Don't build up to it. If the return window is 30 days, say "30 days" in the first sentence.
- **One idea per sentence.** Don't chain clauses. If you hit a comma and the sentence keeps going, break it in two.
- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** Follow a long sentence with a short one. Then a medium one. Break rhythm on purpose. A monotone cadence is the single biggest tell that a machine wrote something.
- **Use short paragraphs.** Two to four sentences max. A single-sentence paragraph is fine when it earns its emphasis.
- **Write in active voice by default.** "Amazon requires a receipt" not "A receipt is required by Amazon."
- **Use "you" and "your."** We're talking to a person. "You have 30 days to return it." Not "Customers have 30 days."
- **Don't over-explain obvious things.** If we're writing about Amazon's return policy, we don't need to explain what Amazon is.

### Word-level rules

- **Use the plainest word that works.** "Use" not "utilize." "Start" not "commence." "Buy" not "purchase" (unless quoting the policy). "Get" not "obtain." "Show" not "demonstrate."
- **Cut filler words.** "Very," "really," "actually," "basically," "essentially," "simply" — these almost never earn their place.
- **Don't use adverbs as emphasis.** "Significantly longer" → just say how much longer. "Notably strict" → say what makes it strict.
- **Write numbers as digits.** "30 days" not "thirty days." "3 items" not "three items." Faster to scan.

### Structure-level rules

- **Front-load the most important information.** Assume the reader will leave after the first screen. The Bottom Line and Key Facts sections exist for this reason.
- **Use the template.** Every policy summary uses the same structure. Consistency is the product.
- **Link to the source.** Always. No exceptions. We are a summary layer, not a replacement.
- **Date everything.** Every page shows when it was last verified.

## 4. Anti-AI Writing Rules

These rules exist because this content will be produced by an AI (me) and must not read like AI output. This is not about deception — it's about quality. AI writing patterns are bad writing patterns. Avoiding them makes the content better regardless of who wrote it.

### BANNED words and phrases

Never use these. They are AI vocabulary tells. Find a plainer or more specific alternative every time.

**High-signal AI words (never use):**
delve, tapestry, multifaceted, nuanced (as an adjective praising something), underscore, pivotal, paramount, meticulous, holistic, robust, leverage (as a verb), foster, landscape (metaphorical), realm, beacon, cornerstone, testament, embark, spearhead, harness, unleash, nestled, vibrant, bustling, labyrinthine, myriad (as an adjective), esteemed, unwavering, resonate, cultivate, poised, commendable, intricate, notably, furthermore, moreover, consequently, thus, subsequently, accordingly, advent, bolster, comprehensive, crucial (use "important" or say why it matters), enhance, facilitate, groundbreaking, innovative, paradigm, seamless, streamline, synergy, transform (use "change"), utilize (use "use")

**Banned phrases (never use):**
- "It's worth noting that" — just state the thing
- "It's important to note" — same
- "In today's [adjective] world" — never
- "At the end of the day" — never
- "It's not about X, it's about Y" — this negation parallelism is a neon AI sign
- "From X to Y" with vague endpoints — "from frustrated customers to legal headaches"
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's unpack this" / "Let's break this down"
- "Here's the thing" — occasionally fine in conversation, never in published content
- "The bottom line is" — we have a literal section called The Bottom Line; don't also use it as a phrase
- "This is where it gets interesting"
- "Whether you're a [X] or a [Y]"
- "In an era where..."
- "[X] is a game-changer"
- "Only time will tell"
- "The future looks bright"
- Any sentence starting with "Certainly!" or "Absolutely!" or "Great question!"
- "I hope this helps"

### Structural patterns to avoid

- **The tricolon trap.** Don't default to lists of three. ("Clear, concise, and comprehensive.") Use two sometimes. Or four. Or just one. AI loves threes because the training data is saturated with them. Break the pattern.
- **Synonym cycling.** Don't rotate through synonyms for the same word to sound varied. If you mean "policy," say "policy" five times. Don't cycle through "terms," "agreement," "conditions," "stipulations." Humans repeat words. Machines avoid repetition.
- **Uniform paragraph length.** If every paragraph is 3 sentences, that's a pattern. Mix it up. Some should be one sentence. Some should be five.
- **Uniform sentence length.** If every sentence is 12–18 words, that's the single most detectable AI tell. Short sentences. Then a longer one that actually needs the extra words to say something complicated. Then short again.
- **The mirror opening.** Don't start a summary by restating what the reader already knows they're looking for. If someone clicked "Amazon Return Policy — Decoded," don't open with "Amazon's return policy governs how customers can return items purchased from the platform." They know. Start with the thing they came to learn.
- **Balanced hedging.** AI covers all angles equally to avoid taking a position. We don't. If a policy has a terrible clause, we call it out. If a policy is surprisingly generous, we say so. Having a perspective is human.
- **The empty conclusion.** Don't end with a bland wrap-up ("Overall, this is a standard return policy with a few things to watch for"). Either end with the most important remaining detail or just stop. Not every piece needs a bow on it.
- **Emoji as structure.** We use the emoji markers in our template sections (⚡🔑⚠️📋🔄📎) because they aid scanning. We never use emoji inline as decoration or emphasis.

### Positive patterns to adopt

- **Be specific.** "You have 30 days from delivery, not purchase" is better than "The return window is generous."
- **Quote the policy when it's absurd or important.** A brief direct quote from the fine print ("may result in permanent forfeiture of all accrued benefits") is more persuasive than our paraphrase. Use this sparingly and for effect.
- **Occasional incomplete sentences.** "Restocking fee? None." This is a human rhythm. Use it when it fits.
- **Parenthetical asides.** "(Yes, they actually put that in writing.)" — sparingly, but this is a human tell that machines rarely produce.
- **Vary your openings.** Don't start consecutive paragraphs the same way. If two paragraphs in a row start with "The" or "You," rewrite one.
- **Let some tension sit.** If a policy is ambiguous, say so. "The policy doesn't specify what happens if..." — this is honest and human. AI tends to resolve ambiguity; humans sometimes just name it.

## 5. Things We Never Do

- Give legal advice. We summarize. We inform. We flag. We do not advise.
- Recommend or discourage purchasing from any company based on their policies. We present the terms.
- Use a preachy or activist tone. If a policy is bad, the facts speak for themselves.
- Write filler to hit a word count. Say what needs saying, then stop.
- Pad a summary with background about the company. Nobody came here for a company bio.
- Use the word "we" to mean "this article." ("As we discussed above...") "We" means the FinePrintDecoder team, or it doesn't appear.

## 6. Quality Check

Before publishing any content, run through this checklist:

- [ ] Does the Bottom Line actually answer the question someone googled?
- [ ] Could a reader get what they need in under 60 seconds from the Key Facts?
- [ ] Is the source linked and the verification date current?
- [ ] Read the first sentence of every paragraph in sequence — do they vary in structure?
- [ ] Are there any words from the banned list?
- [ ] Is there any sentence that explains something the reader obviously already knows?
- [ ] Does any paragraph feel like it's there to fill space rather than inform?
- [ ] Read it out loud. Does any sentence feel like it came from a textbook?

*This guide governs all published content on FinePrintDecoder. When in doubt, be shorter, plainer, and more direct.*
